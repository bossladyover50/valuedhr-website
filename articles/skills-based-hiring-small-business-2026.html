Skills-Based Hiring in 2026: How Small Businesses Can Hire Better Without Requiring a Degree

By Michelle Mendez  •  July 6, 2026  •  5 min read
← Back to Blog

Skills-based hiring has gone from a progressive experiment to the default approach for most employers. 81% of employers used some form of skills-based hiring in 2024, up from 73% in 2023 and 56% in 2022 — and for small businesses competing against companies with bigger budgets and bigger names, it's often the fastest way to fill a role without losing out on candidates who can't check every box on a traditional requirements list.

Why This Trend Is Winning

The case for skills-based hiring isn't just philosophical — the numbers back it up. 92% of employers who adopted skills-based hiring reported finding higher-quality talent, 89% said it was a better predictor of on-the-job success than degrees or resumes, and 92% saw a drop in mis-hires. When companies evaluate candidates on skills rather than titles or degrees, the available talent pool grows nearly 19 times over. For a small business that's been quietly struggling to fill the same role for months, that's not a minor improvement — it's the difference between an empty seat and a hire who works out.

Why It Matters Even More for Small Businesses

47% of small business leaders say finding skilled professionals is harder now than it was a year ago. Small businesses can rarely out-bid larger competitors on salary or brand recognition, which means the traditional playbook — post a job, require a degree and X years of experience, wait for applicants — increasingly filters out exactly the people who could do the job well. Widening the funnel to focus on demonstrated ability instead of credentials is one of the few hiring advantages a small business can create for itself without spending more money.

Start With the Job Description

Most job descriptions are still written as a list of requirements: degree, years of experience, specific software. A skills-based job description flips that — it leads with what the person will actually do and what success in the role looks like, then lists the specific skills and tools needed to get there. Drop the blanket "bachelor's degree required" line unless it's a genuine legal or licensing requirement, and replace vague experience requirements ("5+ years in marketing") with the actual capabilities you're hiring for ("can build and analyze a paid social campaign from scratch").

Rebuild Screening and Interviews Around Proof, Not Resumes

This is where most of the real work happens — employers use skills-based approaches most often at the interview stage (87%) and the screening stage (65%). In practice, that means:

  • Replace resume-only screening with a short work sample, task, or scenario relevant to the role — a mock customer email to respond to, a spreadsheet to clean up, a five-minute walkthrough of how they'd approach a real problem you're facing.
  • Standardize your interview questions. Ask every candidate the same core questions in the same order so you're comparing apples to apples, not vibes.
  • Use behavioral questions that ask candidates for specific examples of when they've demonstrated the skill you're assessing, not hypotheticals.
  • Match the assessment to the role. Hands-on tasks work well for trades and technical roles; short work-sample exercises work well for professional and office roles.

89% of employers using skills-based hiring reported improvements in employee retention compared to their previous hiring methods — the benefit doesn't stop at the offer letter, it shows up in who stays.

What to Watch Out For

The most common mistake is removing the degree requirement from a job posting and calling it done, without building a real skills assessment to replace it. Without a structured process, hiring managers tend to fall back on gut feel — which quietly reintroduces the same bias skills-based hiring is meant to remove. Give whoever is interviewing a simple rubric to score candidates against, and make sure it's the same rubric for everyone who applies.

What Small Business Owners Should Do Now

You don't need to overhaul every job posting this quarter. Pick one role — ideally one that's been hard to fill or has high turnover — and pilot the approach: rewrite the job description around outcomes and skills, build one short work-sample task or scenario question, and standardize the interview questions for every candidate. If it improves the quality of who applies and who gets hired, expand it to your next open role.
